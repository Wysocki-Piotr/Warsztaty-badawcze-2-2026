# Research fundamentals

## Search engines

- **[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/)**: most common search tool. Important fields:
  - Authors (typically, the first one is the *most important* and the last one is his *supervisor*)
  - Source (just below the authors), place where the article is posted (by default, Google Scholar shows the most citated version); if ArXiv, it's *preprint*, if some *proceedings*, the article was peer-reviewed
  - *"Cytowane przez X"* (more citations, more trust you can put in this work)
  - *"Wszystkie wersje"* (if the main version is a preprint, you should double-check for the preceding version)
- **[Google Scholar (Author)](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=HewSBiIAAAAJ&hl=pl)**: overview of the registered author.
  - If you are interested in the author's paper, check if he is experienced in this field or whether it's his first work, etc.
- **Google Scholar (add-ins)**
  - Google Scholar Button - fast citation generation
  - Google Scholar PDF - nice PDF reader with support for checking citations
  - Google Scholar Author Highlighter (or similar)
- **LLMs**
  - There are tools like Consensus in ChatGPT, etc., but just prompting is still okay in most cases

## Conferences & Journals

Conferences are rated via [CORE Ranking](https://portal.core.edu.au/conf-ranks/). Typically, A*/A-rate conferences are high quality. Note that some conferences are not rated - they are too young/too small/too bad to be rated here. Journals do not have this kind of ranking, but they post their Impact Scores, etc., to estimate how good they are.

Note that conferences have workshops - they are for works that are not ready to be fully published or are already published; either way, works on workshops are not considered as high-quality, even if the workshop takes place at the A* conference. Similarly, journals can be branded by big names like "Nature", but this does not always mean they are elite (e.g., "Nature: Scientific Reports" journal has a small impact, but "Nature" is the top-prestige journal with the best papers).

## Good practices

Everyone (even you in this very moment) can publish preprints on sites as ArXiv - thus, the quality of papers on this website might be arbitrary. With enough experience, you can easily distinguish between high-quality research and random slop, but for now you can follow these rules:

- if the paper has proceedings (i.e., is published in a conference/journal), it is a green flag,
- If the paper has ~50 citations or more, it is a green flag.

The citation rule is not perfect - some topics have less interest and fewer citations as a consequence. Surveys, benchmarks, datasets have significantly more citations than pure research papers - and even if they are popular, it might be because "eveyone already use them", not because of the actual quality of the work.
